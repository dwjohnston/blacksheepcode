Recently I asked a neighbour to help me install some new door knobs. I needed to mortise slots for the new knobs to sit in, and I knew that he was a woodworker and had some chisels. 

He happily obliged, and it got me thinking - 'Wouldn't it be nice if there was some way to discover exactly what tools people I know I have?'. 

There's a class of tool that a DIYer doesn't use often enough to really justify owning, but when you need one, they're very useful to have. 

For example, I have a hammerdrill - it's a very effective tool for drilling holes in brick or concrete - useful for adding hooks or shelves to a garage. I've used maybe three times in the time that I've owned it, and I'm glad to have it. 

Another example is that I have some plants to remove, which will require a chainsaw - I can't see that the local Bunnings hires out chainsaws - it would be nice to know which of my neighbours/friends have one. 

So that gets me thinking - what if there was a tool that allowed friends and neighbours to share what tools they have? 

## A bespoke social network won't do

A single purpose social network isn't really going to work here. 

Firstly, I'd have to convince my friends and neighbours to use this one tool. There would be no real reason 

A social network for specific hobbies that people are passionate about works. A good analogous platform that does exist is Board Game Geek - where members can catalouge their collection. 

But BGG provides more than cataloguing a collection, there are forums for dicussing the rules, reviews of the games etc. 

I suppose one could create the BGG equivilent of tools - a site that provides reviews of the tools, DIY advice, etc. 


## Existing platforms could create the tool

Existing platforms like Facebook or LinkedIn could create this tool. 

No doubt they would do a good job. There's plenty to criticise about Facebook, but credit where credit is due, I think they absoultely nail the user experience of their products. 

But there's two problems with say Facebook creating such a tool: 

1. My friends and neighbours aren't necessarily on Facebook. 

The social media landscape is fractured. The golden age of social media was around 2011-2014, where all people you knew in real life you could be found on Facebook, you could follow celebrities on Twitter and so forth.  I liken it to the collapse of the Roman Empire into individual competing kingdoms.

Now, some people I contact via LinkedIn, some people I contact via Facebook, some people I contact via email, others by text and so forth. 

2. A single social media platform creating all of these experiences would lead to janky incoherrent experience. 

A tool catalouge is a niche product, not every user wants or needs it. If Facebook were to create every such tool natively, the platform would become an incorrent mess - like a lot of the enterprise software tools we see. 

## Instead - a pluggable social media

There are two aspects I consider fundamental to 'pluggable' social media

1. Applications (plugins) can be created by third-parties. 

2. Plugins can be used accross multiple social media platforms. 

Essentially the social media platform acts as a kind of identify provider and manages your social network, ie. it's where you control whether you are friends with Bob or not. 

The plugin makes uses of these connections, accross all social social media platforms you might be using the plugin on. 

The plugin in could still allow sharing controls, eg. you might not want to share your list of tools with all of your contacts. 

In the most basic conceptualisation a plugin would not have any concept of platform specific connection types - a connection is just a connection, whether it's a friend on Facebook, a connection on Twitter, or someone you follow on Bluesky.  

For example, this tool cataloging plugin, I could see that I'm friends with Bob on Facebook, Cindy on LinkedIn and so forth.
